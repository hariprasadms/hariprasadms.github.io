The morning of December 31st should have felt hopeful.

The last day of the year.

A fresh start waiting just around the corner.

Instead, I found Amma crying.

She was sitting on the sofa in the living room when I walked in.

The winter sunlight streamed through the windows, filling the room with a soft golden glow.

But nothing about her looked warm.

Nothing about her looked okay.

She was staring at nothing.

Tears silently slipping down her cheeks.

My heart immediately sank.

"Amma?"

She looked up quickly and wiped her eyes.

But it was too late.

I had already seen.

I sat down beside her and gently took her hand.

"What happened?"

She shook her head.

"Nothing."

I knew that answer.

It always meant something.

I moved closer and wrapped an arm around her shoulders.

For a few moments neither of us spoke.

Then she suddenly reached up and held my face.

Her eyes were red.

Tired.

Sad.

The kind of sad that scared me.

"Sanskriti."

Something about the way she said my name made my stomach twist.

I looked at her.

"What is it?"

She swallowed.

"You have to be strong."

My chest tightened immediately.

I hated those words.

I hated them.

Because people only said things like that when they were hurting.

When they were afraid.

When they were preparing for something.

I squeezed her hand tighter.

"Amma..."

"If anything, ever happens to me—"

"No."

The word came out before I could stop it.

Her eyes filled with tears.

Mine did too.

But I refused to let them fall.

"Please don't talk like that."

She tried to smile.

A broken smile.

The kind that made me want to cry even more.

I moved closer and gently stroked her hair.

The way she used to do for me when I was little.

"It's not going to happen."

My voice trembled.

"It won't."

She didn't answer.

She just cried quietly.

And I sat there beside her.

Holding her hand.

Rubbing her back.

Trying desperately to make her feel better even though I was falling apart inside.

Because I couldn't let her see how scared I was.

I couldn't.

She had enough pain already.

Eventually she became calmer.

Her tears slowed.

Her breathing steadied.

And I kissed her forehead.

Just like she had done for me a thousand times growing up.

"I'm going to my room for a bit."

She nodded.

I stood up.

Walked away.

Closed my bedroom door.

And immediately burst into tears.

The sob escaped before I could stop it.

I collapsed onto my bed and buried my face in my pillow.

My entire body shaking.

Because the truth was simple.

I was terrified.

The thought of anything happening to Amma scared me more than anything else in the world.

People didn't understand.

They thought she was just my mother.

But she wasn't.

She was my best friend.

My safe place.

The person who had stayed when everyone else left.

The person who had picked up every broken piece of me.

I couldn't imagine life without her.

And every time she spoke like that, it felt like someone was squeezing my heart.

I stared at the ceiling through blurry eyes.

Trying to calm down.

Trying to breathe.

Trying not to think.

But my mind wandered somewhere I hated.

To my father.

I remembered the long drive.

The one after everything started falling apart.

I had been younger then.

Still confused.

Still hoping there was some explanation.

Some reason why my family was breaking.

He had taken me out alone.

Just the two of us.

And he spent hours talking.

Telling me Amma was mistaken.

Telling me she was believing lies.

Telling me he had never looked at another woman.

Not even once.

Telling me he loved us.

Telling me everything would be okay.

And I believed him.

Because I wanted to.

Because he was my father.

Because daughters believe their fathers.

Then another memory surfaced.

One I understood differently now.

A woman.

Someone he introduced as a colleague.

Someone he claimed he had accidentally run into.

At the time I didn't think much about it.

Now I remembered the smiles.

The familiarity.

The way they seemed to know each other far too well.

The way he kept looking at her.

The way she looked at him.

And suddenly everything made sense.

It hadn't been an accident.

She hadn't been some random colleague.

I knew that now.

The realization made me feel sick.

Because while he was busy convincing me my mother was lying...

He was lying to me too.

I wiped away fresh tears.

But more appeared.

Because the memory that hurt most was still waiting.

The last day.

The final goodbye.

The day I left him.

Forever.

I remembered standing there trying not to cry.

Trying to be brave.

Trying to say goodbye.

My entire heart breaking.

And do you know what he was doing?

He was on the phone.

Talking to her.

His girlfriend.

While I stood there saying goodbye.

While I stood there hoping he would look at me.

Hoping he would hug me.

Hoping he would tell me he loved me.

Hoping he would ask me not to go.

He never did.

He barely looked up.

I still remember standing there waiting.

Waiting for something.

Anything.

A glance.

A smile.

A goodbye.

Instead, he stayed on that call.

And eventually I walked away.

I don't think I've ever fully recovered from that moment. he chose someone else.

Again.

A fresh wave of tears rolled down my face.

I turned onto my side and hugged my pillow.

Trying to stop thinking.

Trying to stop remembering.

But memories never listened.

Neither did the questions.

Questions people asked all the time.

Questions they thought were harmless.

"What does your dad do?"

"Do you see your father often?"

"Are your parents together?"

I hated those questions.

Because everyone expected simple answers.

And I never had any.

Only awkward smiles.

Only explanations.

Only pity.

And I hated pity.

Most of all, I hated watching other girls my age with their fathers.

At school.

At functions.

At restaurants.

On social media.

Laughing together.

Taking pictures.

Holding hands.

Looking happy.

I wasn't jealous of their lives.

I was jealous of something much smaller.

Something much simpler.

The certainty.

The certainty that their father would choose them.

Every single time.

I closed my eyes.

Trying to stop crying.

Trying to stop remembering.

And eventually my thoughts returned to Amma.

And suddenly, mixed in with all the sadness, came pride.

So much pride.

Because if there was one thing my father could never take away from me, it was the admiration I had for my mother.

I remembered those years after the divorce.

The years when money was tight.

The years when she thought I wasn't looking.

The years when she worked herself to exhaustion.

She never complained.

Not once.

She woke up early.

Worked late.

Handled everything alone.

Paid every bill.

Made every decision.

Carried every responsibility.

And somehow still asked me how my day was when I came from school.

Somehow still found the energy to smile at me.

To love me.

To take care of me.

I remembered the day she bought our apartment.

A beautiful three-bedroom apartment.

Ours.

Not rented.

Not borrowed.

Ours.

I had never been prouder in my life.

I remember walking through those empty rooms while Amma stood there trying not to cry.

But I saw it.

The relief.

The pride.

The feeling of finally giving us a home nobody could take away.

And all I could think was:

My mother did this.

By herself.

Without anyone helping her.

Without anyone saving her.

She built a life from nothing.

For us.

For me.

There were times when I later realized she barely had enough money for herself.

Yet somehow, she always made sure I had what I needed.

Books.

School trips.

Birthday gifts.

New clothes.

The little things I never appreciated enough at the time.

I never once felt poor.

Not because we had money.

Because Amma made sure, I never felt the absence of it.

She gave me everything she could.

And then somehow found a way to give more.

A small smile appeared through my tears.

Because when I thought about strength, I didn't think about superheroes.

I thought about her.

A woman who had every reason to give up.

Every reason to become bitter.

Every reason to stop believing in herself.

And yet she stood back up.

Again.

And again.

And again.

Maybe that's why I was so scared of losing her.

Because people like Amma don't come into your life twice.

You only get one.

And I was lucky enough that she was mine.

So, I lay there on the last morning of the year.

Tears still drying on my cheeks.

My heart still aching.

But beneath all the sadness was one truth I knew with complete certainty:

No matter how much my father had disappointed me...

My mother had given me enough love to survive it.

And if I ever became even half the person, she was, I would consider my life a success.
