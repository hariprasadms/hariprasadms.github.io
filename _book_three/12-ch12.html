I stood outside the courtroom, staring at the closed door in front of me.

Just one signature.

One signature and it would finally be over.

Years of marriage.

Years of trying.

Years of crying.

Reduced to a few sheets of paper.

My hands trembled.

Not because I wanted him back.

But because I couldn't believe how much of myself, I had lost trying to save something that had died years ago.

I closed my eyes.

And immediately saw his face.

Kiran.

The man I married.

The man I trusted.

The man who promised to stand beside me for the rest of my life.

And the man who broke me more than anyone else ever could.

A tear slipped down my cheek.

I didn't wipe it away.

I was too tired.

Too tired to pretend anymore.

The worst part wasn't even that he cheated.

It was that he cheated more than once.

The first time, he cried.

He apologized.

He promised it was a mistake.

A moment of weakness.

A stupid decision.

And I believed him.

Because I loved him.

Because I wanted my marriage to survive.

Because Sanskriti was still little.

So, I forgave him.

The second time, it hurt even more.

Because this time, I knew exactly what betrayal felt like.

I knew the sleepless nights that would follow.

I knew the self-doubt.

The humiliation.

The questions.

The endless questions.

What does she have that I don't?

Why am I not enough?

But I stayed.

Again.

For my daughter.

For my family.

For the hope that maybe things would change.

Maybe he would change.

Maybe love would be enough.

I was wrong.

Because then came the third time.

Pallavi.

Even thinking her name made my chest ache.

She was eleven years younger than him.

Eleven.

And somehow my husband had chosen her over the family waiting at home.

Over me.

Over our daughter. Who is thirteen now needs him the most

I still remembered the day I found out.

The messages.

The lies.

The photographs.

The proof I could no longer ignore.

I remembered staring at the screen while my hands shook so badly, I almost dropped my phone.

I remembered feeling physically sick.

I remembered realizing that the marriage I had spent years trying to save existed only in my imagination.

He wasn't sorry.

Not really.

Because people who are truly sorry don't really throw their wife and kid out.

Three times.

Three chances.

Three betrayals.

And somehow, I had survived every single one.

A bitter laugh escaped my lips.

I used to wait for him.

God, I used to wait.

Every night.

Watching the clock.

Listening for his car.

Listening for the front door.

Midnight.

One o'clock.

Two.

Three.

Four.

Sometimes he wouldn't come home until four in the morning.

Sometimes he wouldn’t come home for months.

And every time I would tell myself the same lie.

He's busy.

It's work.

There must be a reason.

Because accepting the truth felt impossible.

Fresh tears blurred my vision.

I remembered sitting alone in the dark room.

The entire house silent.

Everyone asleep.

Except me.

Always me.

Waiting.

Wondering.

Breaking.

And then there were the nights when I couldn't hold myself together anymore.

The nights when the loneliness became unbearable.

The nights when the humiliation felt too heavy.

The nights when I cried until my chest hurt.

Until my eyes burned.

Until I had nothing left.

And somehow, every time, Sanskriti would find me.

My little girl.

My beautiful daughter.

She never demanded answers.

Never asked questions.

She would simply sit beside me.

And I would cry.

Sometimes with my head resting in her lap.

My own child gently stroking my hair while I sobbed.

Trying to comfort her mother.

Trying to put back together someone who was falling apart.

The memory shattered me.

Because she deserved so much better.

No daughter should have to become her mother's strength.

No daughter should have to wipe away her mother's tears.

No daughter should have to watch her mother wonder why she wasn't enough.

I covered my mouth as a sob escaped.

There had been days when I didn't know how to continue.

Days when the pain felt endless.

Days when getting out of bed felt impossible.

Days when I wondered whether life would always feel this empty.

But every time I looked at Sanskriti, I stayed.

For her.

Only her.

Because if she hadn't been there...

I honestly don't know what would have happened to me.

She was the reason I kept breathing.

The reason I kept fighting.

The reason I survived.

Another tear rolled down my cheek.

Today wasn't the day my marriage ended.

That had happened long ago.

The day I learned about Pallavi.

The day I realized the third betrayal hurt no less than the first.

The day I finally understood that love cannot survive where respect no longer exists.

The courtroom door opened.

Someone called my name.

I took a shaky breath.

Then another.

And slowly wiped away my tears.

For years, I had mourned the marriage I thought I had.

Today, I was finally letting go.
