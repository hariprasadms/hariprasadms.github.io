I never imagined my life would come to this.

A cold metal bench.

Grey walls.

A locked cell.

And a silence so heavy it felt impossible to breathe through.

I sat with my back against the wall and stared at the floor.

Three days ago, I had been a husband.

A father.

A successful man living in the UK.

A man who had spent years building a life from scratch.

Today, I was sitting in a jail cell because of a lie.

A domestic abuse complaint.

A complaint filed by the same woman whose affair I had discovered.

Soumya.

My wife.

The woman I had trusted.

The woman I had crossed continents with.

The woman I had built a home with.

The woman who had looked me in the eyes and lied for months.

I closed my eyes.

The memories came anyway.

The messages.

The phone calls.

The photographs.

The proof.

I remembered confronting her.

I remembered the complete lack of guilt in her eyes.

I remembered realizing that the marriage I had fought for no longer existed.

And when I refused to stay quiet...

She destroyed me.

One complaint.

One lie.

And suddenly I was the villain.

I laughed bitterly.

The sound echoed through the empty cell.

Funny.

The person who had cheated was free.

The person who had been cheated on was sitting behind bars.

I rubbed my face.

Exhausted.

Broken.

Angry.

But beneath all of that was something worse.

Regret.

So much regret.

Because this wasn't even the first time.

Years earlier, I had caught Soumya cheating.

The first time.

I still remembered how she cried.

How she apologized.

How she promised it would never happen again.

And like an idiot, I believed her.

Because Sana was young.

Because I wanted my family to survive.

Because I didn't want my daughter growing up in a broken home.

So, I forgave her.

And for a while, I convinced myself things had improved.

That we had moved past it.

That people could change.

But they hadn't.

The second affair wasn't a mistake.

It was a choice.

Just like the first one.

The difference was that this time, when I discovered the truth, she decided to protect herself by destroying me.

A false accusation.

A false story.

And suddenly years of hard work meant nothing.

Everything disappeared overnight.

I leaned my head back against the wall.

And then I thought about Sana.

My daughter.

My beautiful little girl.

The thought alone nearly broke me.

She was eleven.

Too young to understand lawyers and courts and false accusations.

Too young to understand why her father wasn't coming home.

The thought made my chest ache.

A tear slipped down my cheek.

Then another.

I didn't bother wiping them away.

There was no one here to see.

No one here to judge.

I could finally stop pretending to be strong.

The image of Sana's face filled my mind.

The first time I held her.

The first time she called me Dad.

The way she used to run into my arms after school.

The way she would hold my hand while crossing the road even when she insisted she was too old for it.

God.

I missed her.

More than anything.

More than my freedom.

More than my pride.

I missed my daughter.

I lowered my head into my hands.

And for the first time since all of this began, I cried.

Not for the marriage.

Not for Soumya.

Not even for myself.

I cried because everything I had spent years building was collapsing around me.

The tears wouldn't stop.

And somewhere between the anger and the heartbreak and the regret, another thought appeared.

A painful one.

The life I could have had.

The road I never took.

The people I lost.

The choices I couldn't undo.

I thought about how quickly I had married Soumya.

How desperately I had wanted to move forward.

How desperately I had wanted to stop looking backward.

To stop remembering someone I could never have.

Radhe.

Even after all those years, her name still hurt.

I wasn't deeply in love with Soumya when I married her.

I cared for her.

Respected her.

Believed I could build a future with her.

But if I was honest with myself, part of me had rushed into that marriage because I was tired of carrying old heartbreak.

And now I was paying for that decision.

I closed my eyes.

And for the first time in years, I allowed myself to think about her.

I wondered where she was.

I wondered if she was happy.

If life had been kind to her.

If she had the family she dreamed of.

If she still laughed the same way.

I hoped she was happy.

I truly did.

Because no matter how much time had passed, some part of me still wanted good things for her.

Even if those good things had nothing to do with me.

A sad smile crossed my face.

I hadn't spoken her name aloud in years.

Yet here I was, sitting in a jail cell on the other side of the world, thinking about her.

Thinking about what could have been.

Thinking about the version of my life that never happened.

***

The truth came out quickly.

Much quicker than anyone expected.

Within a few days, the evidence was examined.

The messages.

The records.

The inconsistencies in her statements.

Everything.

Piece by piece, the lies unravelled.

And just like that, I was proven innocent.

The charges fell apart.

The accusations were shown for what they were.

False.

I walked out of that station carrying nothing but exhaustion and a heart that felt ten years older.

I should have felt victorious.

Instead, I just felt tired.

Because innocence couldn't give me back the peace I had lost.

***

Months later, the divorce was finalized.

Years of marriage ended with signatures and silence.

No apologies.

No explanations.

No closure.

Just the end.

The only thing that mattered was Sana.

And thankfully, the court agreed.

We were granted shared custody.

Half the week she would be with me.

Half the week with her mother.

It wasn't the future I had imagined when she was born.

But it meant I wouldn't lose her.

It meant I could still hear her laugh.

Still help with homework.

Still take her out on weekends.

Still watch her grow up.

Still be her father.

The marriage was over.

The accusations were gone.

The scars remained.

But every time Sana ran into my arms, I was reminded of something important.

Some things are worth fighting for.

And she always would always be.
