A story about software testing in the age of AI

From Bugs to Brilliance

One engineer joins a software company believing testers just click buttons. One production outage later, he begins the journey that will redefine his career — and how his whole team thinks about quality.

by Hariprasad Srinivas · SDET Experts · promptqa.dev

CHAPTER 01 — The Day Everything Changed
Page 1 · First day

The rain tapped gently against the glass walls of TechNova Solutions. Arjun stood in the reception area holding a laptop bag that still smelled new. Today was his first day as a Software Engineer. After years of studying and interviews, he had finally made it.

Around him he noticed developers, managers, and testers. He secretly believed testers only clicked buttons. He wanted to be a developer — not a tester. Little did he know that testing would completely change his career.

Page 2 · Induction

The induction session began. A senior engineering manager wrote four words on the board:

"Quality is everyone's responsibility."

Arjun disagreed. Wasn't quality the testing team's job? The manager explained that customers never care who introduced a bug. They only know the product failed.

Years later, Arjun would repeat this same lesson to hundreds of engineers.

Page 3 · The deploy

Three weeks later, Arjun modified a login feature. The task seemed simple: increase the username length and release the change. Everything worked in his local testing. The code passed review and was deployed.

[Sat 02:14:51] auth-service login requests: elevated error rate [Sat 02:16:33] auth-service ERROR ValidationException: username exceeds limit [Sat 02:17:02] pagerduty SEV-1 · LOGIN DOWN affected users: 12,400+ [Sat 02:17:40] on-call escalating… emergency calls started across the company

At 2:17 AM on Saturday morning, thousands of banking customers could not log in.

Page 4 · The war room

Monday morning, the war room was full. Customer complaints, logs, and alerts covered giant screens. Everyone explained their perspective. Then a senior QA engineer named Meera asked one simple question:

"What happens when a username exceeds fifty characters?"

Nobody knew. Within minutes, they discovered a missing validation check.

Page 5 · Coffee with Meera

One defect. One missed test. Thousands of impacted customers. Arjun realised software wasn't only code — it affected real people.

After the meeting, Meera invited him for coffee. She didn't blame him. Instead she asked, "Do you know why this happened?"

Arjun replied, "Because I wrote bad code."

Meera smiled. "No. You only tested that it worked. You never tested how it could fail."

// Margin note Developers prove software works. Testers prove where it breaks. Great engineers learn to do both.
Page 6 · How testers think

The next day, Meera introduced three ideas: positive testing, negative testing, and boundary testing.

"If a field accepts 1 to 50 characters, where should you test?"

Arjun answered, "25 characters."

Meera laughed. "Most defects live at the boundaries: 0, 1, 50 and 51."

The middle rarely fails. The edges almost always do.

For the first time, Arjun understood how testers think differently.

Page 7 · Investigation

That afternoon, Arjun explored a registration form. He intentionally entered invalid data, long values, and unusual combinations. Within an hour, he discovered three defects — in a feature that had already passed developer testing.

Testing suddenly felt less like checking and more like investigation.

Page 8 · Curiosity becomes habit

Weeks passed and curiosity became a habit. Arjun carried a notebook and constantly asked questions: What if the network fails? What if two users submit at the same time? What if data is corrupted?

Meera explained that great testers are not necessarily the most technical people. They are often the most curious people.

Page 9 · The folder

One Friday evening, Meera dropped a folder onto his desk. "Ready for your next challenge?" she asked.

Inside was a production defect involving APIs. Arjun had heard the term but never really understood it. The next stage of his journey was about to begin.
