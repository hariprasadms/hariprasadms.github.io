Page 1 · The demo

The new tool arrived with a slick demo. It was powered by AI — software that can read code and write text, including tests, almost like a person. In the demo, it read a feature and wrote a hundred tests in ten seconds. The room gasped.

Rohan leaned back, arms crossed, almost smiling. "Well, that's it for the testing team. Why pay humans to write tests when a machine does it in seconds?"

Page 2 · Dazzled

Arun was the most excited of all. That afternoon he pointed the AI at a brand-new feature and typed a simple request: "Write tests for this."

AI testing assistant > generated 512 tests in 41 seconds > run result: 512 passed · 0 failed

Five hundred tests. All green. In under a minute. Work that would have taken him a week. He stared at the wall of passes and felt like he had been handed a superpower.

Page 3 · Ship it

He didn't read all 512 tests. Who could? They were green. Green meant good. He attached them to the feature, wrote "full AI test coverage ✅" in the notes, and shipped it.

It felt like the easiest win of his career. Somewhere at the back of his mind, a much older memory tried to raise its hand — it worked on my machine — but he was too pleased to listen.

Page 4 · The wolf, in a new coat

Nine days later, a customer reported a broken discount. A valid code was being rejected. A real bug, in the exact feature with "full AI test coverage." All 512 tests were still green.

Arun felt a cold, familiar drop in his stomach. He had been here before — a 2 AM outage, a midnight stakeout. He opened the AI tests and actually read them for the first time.

Page 5 · Green, and empty

What he found made him sit down slowly. The tests ran the feature — but barely checked anything. Many had no real assertion at all. Remember: the assertion is the line that decides what "pass" means. Without it, a test passes as long as the code doesn't crash.

# what the AI wrote call applyDiscount("SAVE20") assert response is not empty ← passes for almost anything # what it should have checked assert finalPrice == £80.00 ← the thing that actually matters

Page 6 · It tested that it ran

It was the same lesson from his early days, wearing a new coat. The AI had checked that the code ran, not that it was right. Five hundred tests confirming the discount feature responded — and not one confirming the discount was actually applied.

"It's the duplicate-payment bug all over again," Arun muttered. "Payment taken, order not created. The thing ran. Nobody checked the result." The AI had written 512 ways of saying "it didn't crash."

Page 7 · The most dangerous green

And there was worse. A few AI tests had simply assumed how the feature worked and tested their own wrong assumptions. They weren't just empty. They were confidently checking the wrong thing.

The green wall had felt like safety. It had been a painting of safety. The most dangerous test of all, Arun realised, is one that passes for the wrong reason.

// The line worth keeping The AI gave me a thousand tests that tested nothing. Green is not the same as safe.

Page 8 · The oldest lesson

Meera didn't say "I told you so." She just sat down with the same patience as years ago. "What did you actually do here?"

Arun answered slowly, because he could hear his first week echoing back. "I trusted that it worked. I never checked how it could fail." He looked up. "I made the exact mistake from day one. I just made it five hundred times faster."

"Yes," Meera said gently. "Speed doesn't fix bad thinking. It multiplies it."

Page 9 · The wrong debate

In the retro, Rohan was triumphant. "See? AI testing is a toy. Humans are still the only real testers."

But Arun, staring at his notebook, felt that both sides of the office were asking the wrong question. It wasn't "human or machine." The AI hadn't failed because it was useless. It had failed because he had used it with his eyes closed — like a passenger asleep while the car drove itself.

"It's not that AI can't test," he said quietly. "It's that I didn't know how to use it yet."
