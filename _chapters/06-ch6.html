Page 1 · Green, and still broken

The new checkout feature was perfect. Every test passed. The pipeline was a wall of green. Arun signed it off with pride. Then came the winter sale.

At 9 AM, thousands of shoppers arrived at once. By 9:04, the site slowed to a crawl. By 9:11, it fell over completely. No error in the code. No failing test. The feature worked — it just couldn't survive a crowd.

Page 2 · Two kinds of question

Meera drew a line down the whiteboard. "Every test you've written so far asks one question: does the feature work? That's functional testing. But there's a second question, and you never asked it: does it work well? That's non-functional testing."

Two different questions
Functional: does it work?
Non-functional: does it work WELL?

The first kept passing. The second was never asked.

Page 3 · Testing the crowd

"Your checkout works for one user," Meera said. "Did anyone test it with ten thousand at once?" That is performance testing — checking that software stays fast and stable under real-world load.

She named the family: a load test checks a normal busy day; a stress test pushes until it breaks, to learn where the ceiling is. "You don't want to discover your ceiling during a sale. You want to discover it on a Tuesday, on purpose."

Page 4 · Ten thousand fake shoppers

Arun built a test that pretended to be 10,000 shoppers. The numbers told the story.

100 users … page loads in 0.3s 5,000 users … page loads in 2.1s 10,000 users … page loads in 31s, then crash

The trouble was one slow database query, fine for one user, deadly for ten thousand. Fixing it let the system grow smoothly with the crowd — its ability to do that is called scalability.

Page 5 · The windows, again

Then Meera asked a colder question. "Your checkout takes money. Have you tested it against people who want to steal?" That is security testing.

Arun remembered the negative payment he'd uncovered months earlier — that had been a security bug too. Meera showed him another: typing sneaky commands into a form to trick the system, an attack called injection. "Rule one of security," she said. "Never trust anything a user sends you. The screen is the front door. Attackers still come through the windows."

Page 6 · Software for everyone

The next lesson surprised him most. Meera turned off her mouse and used the site with only the keyboard. She couldn't finish checkout — a key button could not be reached. Then she switched on a screen reader, the software a blind person uses to hear the page aloud. It read a button as "button, button, button." Useless.

This is accessibility — making sure software works for people with disabilities. "Some of your users can't see the screen, or can't use a mouse," Meera said. "Leaving them out isn't just poor quality. In many countries, it's against the law."

Page 7 · What if a part dies?

One more question. "What happens when something your app depends on stops responding — a payment provider, a database?" That is reliability: staying usable even when a piece breaks.

They tested it by switching off a service on purpose. The app didn't just slow down — it showed customers a frightening error and lost their basket. They fixed it to fail gently: keep the basket, show a calm message, try again. "Things will break," Meera said. "Good software breaks politely."

Page 8 · The many rooms of quality

Arun wrote them all in his notebook. Speed. Scale. Security. Accessibility. Reliability. Together, testers call these the quality attributes — all the ways software must be good, beyond simply working.

// The line worth keeping Working software and good software are not the same thing.

Page 9 · Humbled, again

He had felt like an expert. The sale-day crash reminded him he had only been testing one room of a very large house. There were whole floors he had never entered.

It didn't discourage him. It did the opposite. Three years ago he thought testers clicked buttons. Now he knew the field was deep enough to spend a career in — and he wanted to.
